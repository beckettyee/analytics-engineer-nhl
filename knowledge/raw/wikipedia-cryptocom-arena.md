Crypto.com Arena

**Crypto.com Arena** (originally and colloquially known as **Staples Center**) is a multi-purpose indoor [arena](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arena "Arena") in [downtown Los Angeles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Downtown_Los_Angeles "Downtown Los Angeles"). Opened on October 17, 1999, as Staples Center, it is located next to the [Los Angeles Convention Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Los_Angeles_Convention_Center "Los Angeles Convention Center") complex along [Figueroa Street](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Figueroa_Street "Figueroa Street"), and has since been considered a part of [L.A. Live](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L.A._Live "L.A. Live"). Owned and operated by [Anschutz Entertainment Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anschutz_Entertainment_Group "Anschutz Entertainment Group") (AEG), it is the home venue of the [Los Angeles Lakers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Los_Angeles_Lakers "Los Angeles Lakers") of the [National Basketball Association](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Basketball_Association "National Basketball Association") (NBA) and [Los Angeles Kings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Los_Angeles_Kings "Los Angeles Kings") of the [National Hockey League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Hockey_League "National Hockey League") (NHL)—which are both owned in part by AEG's founder [Philip Anschutz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philip_Anschutz "Philip Anschutz"), as well as the [WNBA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WNBA "WNBA")'s [Los Angeles Sparks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Los_Angeles_Sparks "Los Angeles Sparks").

The [Los Angeles Clippers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Los_Angeles_Clippers "Los Angeles Clippers") also played in the arena from [1999](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1999%E2%80%932000_NBA_season "1999–2000 NBA season") to [2024](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2023%E2%80%9324_NBA_season "2023–24 NBA season"), before leaving for their new arena, [Intuit Dome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intuit_Dome "Intuit Dome"), located in [Inglewood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inglewood,_California "Inglewood, California").

From 1999 to 2024, it was the only arena in the NBA shared by two teams, as well as one of only three North American professional sports venues (alongside [SoFi Stadium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SoFi_Stadium "SoFi Stadium") in nearby Inglewood, and New Jersey's [MetLife Stadium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MetLife_Stadium "MetLife Stadium")) to have hosted two teams from the same league. The venue is also frequently used for major concerts, and has been the most frequent host of the [Grammy Awards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grammy_Awards "Grammy Awards") ceremony since its opening.

Crypto.com Arena will host the artistic gymnastics competition and boxing finals during the [2028 Summer Olympics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2028_Summer_Olympics "2028 Summer Olympics").

## Description

\[ [edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Crypto.com_Arena&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Description")\]

Crypto.com Arena has 950,000 square feet (88,257.9 m2) of total space, with a 94-foot (28.7 m) by 200-foot (61.0 m) arena floor. It stands 150 feet (45.7 m) tall.[\[6\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-LASportsCouncil-6) The arena seats up to 19,067 for [basketball](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basketball "Basketball"), 18,145 for [ice hockey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice_hockey "Ice hockey"), and around 20,000 for [concerts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concert "Concert") or other sporting events.[\[1\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-staplescenterseating-1)[\[7\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-AEGstaplescenter-7) Two-thirds of the arena's seating, including 2,500 club seats, are in the lower bowl. There are also 160 luxury suites, including 15 event suites, on three levels between the lower and upper bowls.[\[6\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-LASportsCouncil-6) The arena's attendance record is held by the fight between World [WBA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Boxing_Association "World Boxing Association") [Welterweight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Welterweight "Welterweight") Champion [Antonio Margarito](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antonio_Margarito "Antonio Margarito") and [Shane Mosley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shane_Mosley "Shane Mosley") with a crowd of 20,820, set on January 25, 2009.[\[8\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-LAT-8)

## History

\[ [edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Crypto.com_Arena&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: History")\]

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Staples_Center_Lakers.jpg/250px-Staples_Center_Lakers.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Staples_Center_Lakers.jpg) During a Lakers pre-game warm-up prior to the installation of the new scoreboard, and after the implementation of a new lighting system

The arena has been referred to as "the deal that almost wasn't."[\[20\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-20)[\[21\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-:02-21) Long before construction broke ground, plans for the arena were negotiated between elected city officials and [real estate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_estate "Real estate") developers [Edward P. Roski](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_P._Roski "Edward P. Roski") of Majestic Realty and [Philip Anschutz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philip_Anschutz "Philip Anschutz").[\[22\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-:12-22) Roski and Anschutz had acquired the [Los Angeles Kings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Los_Angeles_Kings "Los Angeles Kings") in 1995 and in 1996 began looking for a new home for their team, which then played at the [Great Western Forum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kia_Forum "Kia Forum") in [Inglewood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inglewood,_California "Inglewood, California").[\[23\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-23)[\[24\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-24)

Majestic Realty Co. and [AEG](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anschutz_Entertainment_Group "Anschutz Entertainment Group") were scouring the Los Angeles area for available land to develop an arena when they were approached by [Steve Soboroff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steve_Soboroff "Steve Soboroff"), then president of the LA Recreation and Parks Commission. Soboroff requested that they consider building the arena in [downtown Los Angeles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Downtown_Los_Angeles "Downtown Los Angeles") adjacent to the convention center. This proposal intrigued Roski and Anschutz, and soon a plan to develop the arena was devised.[\[22\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-:12-22)

Months of negotiations ensued between Anschutz and city officials, with Roski and John Semcken of Majestic Realty Co. spearheading the negotiations for the real estate developers. The negotiations grew contentious at times and the real estate developers threatened to pull out altogether on more than one occasion.[\[22\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-:12-22) The main opposition came from Councilman [Joel Wachs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joel_Wachs "Joel Wachs"), who opposed utilizing public funds to subsidize the proposed project,[\[21\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-:02-21)[\[25\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-25) and councilwoman [Rita Walters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rita_Walters "Rita Walters"), who objected to parts of it.[\[26\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-26)

Ultimately, the developers and city leaders reached an agreement, and in 1997, construction broke ground on the new building, which opened two years later. It was financed privately at a cost of US$375 million and was named for the office-supply company [Staples, Inc.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Staples,_Inc. "Staples, Inc."), which was one of the center's corporate sponsors that paid for naming rights.[\[6\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-LASportsCouncil-6) Staples' 20-year naming rights deal was renewed in 2009.[\[27\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-:0-27) The arena opened on October 17, 1999, with a [Bruce Springsteen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bruce_Springsteen "Bruce Springsteen") & The [E Street Band](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E_Street_Band "E Street Band") concert as its inaugural event.[\[28\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-28) The first Kings games at the arena was on October 20, 1999 where the Kings tied 2–2 in overtime against the [Boston Bruins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boston_Bruins "Boston Bruins"). The first Clippers game at the arena was on November 2, 1999 where the Clippers lost 92–104 to the [Seattle SuperSonics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seattle_SuperSonics "Seattle SuperSonics"). The first Lakers game at the arena was on November 3, 1999 where the Lakers won 103–88 against the [Vancouver Grizzlies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vancouver_Grizzlies "Vancouver Grizzlies").

On October 21, 2009, the arena celebrated its tenth anniversary.[\[29\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-StaplesAnniversary3-29) To commemorate the occasion, the venue's official web site nominated 25 of the arena's greatest moments from its first ten years with fans voting on the top ten.[\[30\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-StaplesCenter253-30)[\[31\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-StaplesCenterMoments3-31)

The [Los Angeles Avengers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Los_Angeles_Avengers "Los Angeles Avengers") of the [Arena Football League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arena_Football_League "Arena Football League") (AFL) and the [South Bay Lakers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Bay_Lakers "South Bay Lakers") of the [NBA G League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NBA_G_League "NBA G League") were also tenants of Staples Center; the Avengers folded in 2009, and the D-Fenders moved to the Lakers' practice facility at the [Toyota Sports Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toyota_Sports_Center "Toyota Sports Center") in [El Segundo, California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/El_Segundo,_California "El Segundo, California") for the 2011–12 season.[\[6\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-LASportsCouncil-6)

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Penguins_%40_Kings_%2830737094236%29.jpg/250px-Penguins_%40_Kings_%2830737094236%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Penguins_@_Kings_(30737094236).jpg) The arena in 2016, prior to a Kings game

During the 2010 NBA and NHL offseason, the arena was renovated with refurbished locker rooms for the Lakers, Kings, and Clippers, and the installation of a new [US$](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_dollar "United States dollar") 10 million [HD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-definition_video "High-definition video") center-hung video scoreboard and [jumbotron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jumbotron "Jumbotron"), replacing the original one that had been in place since the building opened in 1999.[\[32\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-Live4HD3-32) The [Panasonic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panasonic "Panasonic") Live 4HD scoreboard was officially unveiled on September 22, 2010, as AEG and Staples Center executives, as well as player representatives from the Lakers ( [Sasha Vujacic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sasha_Vujacic "Sasha Vujacic")), Clippers ( [Craig Smith](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Craig_Smith_(basketball,_born_1983) "Craig Smith (basketball, born 1983)")), and Kings ( [Matt Greene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matt_Greene "Matt Greene")) were on hand for the presentation.

On January 15, 2018, in the aftermath of an NBA basketball game between the [Houston Rockets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Houston_Rockets "Houston Rockets") and the [Los Angeles Clippers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Los_Angeles_Clippers "Los Angeles Clippers"), point guard [Chris Paul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chris_Paul "Chris Paul") utilized a secret tunnel (connecting the away team's locker room to the backdoor of the Clippers locker room) to confront former Clipper teammates [Austin Rivers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Austin_Rivers "Austin Rivers") and [Blake Griffin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blake_Griffin "Blake Griffin"). Paul was joined by teammates [Trevor Ariza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trevor_Ariza "Trevor Ariza"), [James Harden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Harden "James Harden"), and [Gerald Green](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gerald_Green "Gerald Green") to confront the opponents, which only resulted in verbal altercations.[\[33\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-33)

Following the death of [Kobe Bryant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kobe_Bryant "Kobe Bryant"), his daughter Gianna, and seven others in a [helicopter crash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2020_Calabasas_helicopter_crash "2020 Calabasas helicopter crash") in January 2020, a number of media outlets picked up on a phrase used by some, referring to the arena as "The House That Kobe Built", due to his historic 20-year career with the Lakers.[\[34\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-34)[\[35\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-35) On August 24, 2020, Los Angeles City Council president [Herb Wesson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herb_Wesson "Herb Wesson") announced a proposal to rename the stretch of Figueroa Street around Staples Center to " [Kobe Bryant Boulevard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kobe_Bryant_Boulevard "Kobe Bryant Boulevard")".[\[36\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-36)

In September 2021, the Clippers broke ground on a new arena in [Inglewood, California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inglewood,_California "Inglewood, California"), known as [Intuit Dome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intuit_Dome "Intuit Dome"), which became its new home arena in 2024.[\[37\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-:7-37)[\[38\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-38)

On November 16, 2021, it was announced that the naming rights to Staples Center had been acquired by Singapore-based [cryptocurrency exchange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryptocurrency_exchange "Cryptocurrency exchange") [Crypto.com](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com "Crypto.com"), renaming it Crypto.com Arena effective December 25, 2021 (with the changeover coinciding with the Lakers' nationally televised [Christmas Day game](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Basketball_Association_Christmas_games "National Basketball Association Christmas games")). The deal was reported to be valued at $700 million over 20 years, in comparison to the $116 million paid by Staples under its previous 20-year agreement—making it the most valuable naming rights contract in all of sports.[\[39\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-39)[\[27\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-:0-27) The name change was [met with opposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Criticism_of_advertising "Criticism of advertising") and many fans still refer to this arena as Staples Center.[\[40\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-40) Former Lakers star [Shaquille O'Neal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shaquille_O%27Neal "Shaquille O'Neal") stated on his podcast that he was "glad" that the arena's name had been changed, as he felt that "Staples Center belongs to Shaq and Kobe forever."[\[41\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-41)

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/UCTER_section_at_Crypto.com_Arena.jpg/250px-UCTER_section_at_Crypto.com_Arena.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:UCTER_section_at_Crypto.com_Arena.jpg)

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Lakers_NBA_Cup_court_2024.jpg/250px-Lakers_NBA_Cup_court_2024.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Lakers_NBA_Cup_court_2024.jpg)

2024 renovations at Crypto.com Arena - featuring the new UCTER section (left) and new hanging scoreboard (right)

In 2022, the arena began to undergo a multi-phase renovation, expected to be completed in 2024; the first phase over the 2022 NBA and NHL offseason included new video boards and ribbon displays, and updated concessions. There are plans for the City View Terrace to be converted into an indoor outdoor deck, a new area known as the Tunnel Club, Chick Hearn Court to be converted into a pedestrian plaza between the arena and the rest of L.A. Live, and updated player facilities such as locker rooms.[\[42\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-42)

### Music

\[ [edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Crypto.com_Arena&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Music")\]

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Staples_Center_in_2006.jpg/250px-Staples_Center_in_2006.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Staples_Center_in_2006.jpg) Outside the arena in 2006

[Bruce Springsteen & The E Street Band](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bruce_Springsteen_%26_The_E_Street_Band "Bruce Springsteen & The E Street Band") were the first act to perform at the venue on its opening in 1999. [Dave Matthews Band](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dave_Matthews_Band "Dave Matthews Band") famously played the venue twice in 2008, despite the first show being the day of founding member and saxophonist [LeRoi Moore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LeRoi_Moore "LeRoi Moore")'s death.[\[43\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-43)

After the American singer [Michael Jackson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Jackson "Michael Jackson") died in 2009, [a televised memorial service](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Jackson_memorial_service "Michael Jackson memorial service") was held at the arena. Its operator, AEG, had promoted the [This Is It](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/This_Is_It_(concert_residency) "This Is It (concert residency)") concert residency that Jackson had been scheduled to perform at [The O2 Arena](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_O2_Arena "The O2 Arena") in London.[\[44\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-44) Jackson had been rehearsing at the arena in the weeks prior to his death; he last had rehearsed there approximately 12 hours before his death (on the same day).

It hosted the [1st Annual Latin Grammy Awards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1st_Annual_Latin_Grammy_Awards "1st Annual Latin Grammy Awards") in 2000 and the [2012 MTV Video Music Awards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2012_MTV_Video_Music_Awards "2012 MTV Video Music Awards").[\[45\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-45) In 2011, [Jenni Rivera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jenni_Rivera "Jenni Rivera") became the first female [regional Mexican](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regional_Mexican "Regional Mexican") musician to perform a sell-out at the arena.[\[46\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-46)

[Lady Gaga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lady_Gaga "Lady Gaga") has performed three nights of her [Monster Ball Tour](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monster_Ball_Tour "Monster Ball Tour") at Crypto.com Arena, August 11–12, 2011. Later on brang the third date March 28, 2011 celebrating her birthday at the arena. On January 20–21, 2013 she brang her [Born This Way Ball Tour](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Born_This_Way_Ball_Tour "Born This Way Ball Tour"). On July 21–22, 2014 she brang her [artRAVE: The ARTPOP Ball](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArtRAVE:_The_ARTPOP_Ball "ArtRAVE: The ARTPOP Ball") marking her last shows at this arena.

[Taylor Swift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taylor_Swift "Taylor Swift") has performed 16 sellouts at Crypto.com Arena—the most of any performer at the venue.[\[47\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-:1-47) On August 21, 2015, prior to one of her performances on the [1989 World Tour](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_1989_World_Tour "The 1989 World Tour"), Kobe Bryant presented Swift with a banner commemorating this achievement, which was hung in the arena's rafters. The Taylor Swift banner, however, became the subject of a [curse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sports-related_curses "Sports-related curses") among Lakers and Kings fans, who suspected that the banner was contributing to their teams' respective playoff droughts. Eventually, the Kings began to hide the Taylor Swift banner during home games, and the banner was taken down entirely in December 2020.[\[48\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-48)[\[47\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-:1-47)

Mexican musicians [Gloria Trevi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gloria_Trevi "Gloria Trevi") and [Alejandra Guzmán](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alejandra_Guzm%C3%A1n "Alejandra Guzmán") played two sellout shows at the arena in 2017.[\[49\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-49) Rapper [Nipsey Hussle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nipsey_Hussle "Nipsey Hussle")'s memorial service was held at the venue on April 11, 2019.[\[50\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-50) As part of [Super Bowl LVI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Super_Bowl_LVI "Super Bowl LVI") festivities, the arena hosted the "Super Bowl Music Fest" in February 2022, headlined by [Halsey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halsey_(singer) "Halsey (singer)"), [Machine Gun Kelly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Machine_Gun_Kelly_(musician) "Machine Gun Kelly (musician)"), [Blake Shelton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blake_Shelton "Blake Shelton") and [Gwen Stefani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gwen_Stefani "Gwen Stefani"), [Miley Cyrus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miley_Cyrus "Miley Cyrus"), and [Green Day](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_Day "Green Day").[\[51\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-51)

### Sports

\[ [edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Crypto.com_Arena&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Sports")\]

The venue opened in 1999 as the home of the [Los Angeles Lakers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Los_Angeles_Lakers "Los Angeles Lakers") and [Los Angeles Clippers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Los_Angeles_Clippers "Los Angeles Clippers") (NBA), and [Los Angeles Kings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Los_Angeles_Kings "Los Angeles Kings") of the NHL. The [Los Angeles Sparks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Los_Angeles_Sparks "Los Angeles Sparks") of the WNBA joined in 2001, while the [Los Angeles D-Fenders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Los_Angeles_D-Fenders "Los Angeles D-Fenders") of the NBA D-League joined in 2006. It became home to the [Los Angeles Avengers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Los_Angeles_Avengers "Los Angeles Avengers") of the Arena Football League in 2000 until the team's discontinuation in 2009.[\[52\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-TeamsEvents-52)

On June 21, 2003, the arena hosted the boxing event [Lennox Lewis vs. Vitali Klitschko](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lennox_Lewis_vs._Vitali_Klitschko "Lennox Lewis vs. Vitali Klitschko"), which was [Lennox Lewis'](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lennox_Lewis "Lennox Lewis") final title defense before retiring. Klitschko was leading on the scorecards however the fight ended as a 6th-round [TKO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TKO "TKO") due to a sustained cut over Klitschko's eye.

Since its opening day, the arena has hosted seven [NBA Finals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NBA_Finals "NBA Finals") series with the Lakers, the [2012](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2012_Stanley_Cup_Final "2012 Stanley Cup Final") and [2014 Stanley Cup Final](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2014_Stanley_Cup_Final "2014 Stanley Cup Final"), five [WNBA Finals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WNBA_Finals "WNBA Finals"), the 2002 [U.S. Figure Skating Championships](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Figure_Skating_Championships "United States Figure Skating Championships"), the [52nd](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/52nd_National_Hockey_League_All-Star_Game "52nd National Hockey League All-Star Game") and [62nd](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/62nd_National_Hockey_League_All-Star_Game "62nd National Hockey League All-Star Game") NHL All-Star game, three [NBA All-Star Games](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NBA_All-Star_Game "NBA All-Star Game") ( [2004](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2004_NBA_All-Star_Game "2004 NBA All-Star Game"), [2011](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2011_NBA_All-Star_Game "2011 NBA All-Star Game") and [2018](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2018_NBA_All-Star_Game "2018 NBA All-Star Game")), the [Pac-10 Conference 104](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UFC_104 "UFC 104") in 2009, [UFC 184](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UFC_184 "UFC 184") in 2015, [UFC 227](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UFC_227 "UFC 227") in 2018, the [2009 World Figure Skating Championships](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2009_World_Figure_Skating_Championships "2009 World Figure Skating Championships"), the [Summer X Games](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X_Games "X Games") indoor competitions (2003–13), and several [HBO Championship Boxing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HBO_Boxing "HBO Boxing") matches.[\[7\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-AEGstaplescenter-7)[\[53\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-53)

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/ClippersStaples.JPG/250px-ClippersStaples.JPG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:ClippersStaples.JPG) Before a Clippers game in March 2011

On January 22, 2006, Los Angeles Lakers player [Kobe Bryant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kobe_Bryant "Kobe Bryant") scored a career-high 81 points in the Crypto.com Arena against the [Toronto Raptors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toronto_Raptors "Toronto Raptors"), the [second-highest number of points scored in a single game](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_National_Basketball_Association_players_with_60_or_more_points_in_a_game "List of National Basketball Association players with 60 or more points in a game") in NBA history,[\[54\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-81-Point-Game_Highlights-54) second only to [Wilt Chamberlain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilt_Chamberlain "Wilt Chamberlain")'s [100-point performance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilt_Chamberlain%27s_100-point_game "Wilt Chamberlain's 100-point game"). Of the team's six NBA championships since moving to the venue, the Lakers have celebrated their [2000](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2000_NBA_Finals "2000 NBA Finals") and [2010](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2010_NBA_Finals "2010 NBA Finals") victories at Crypto.com Arena with series-winning victories at home.

Prior to the [2006–07 NBA season](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2006%E2%80%9307_NBA_season "2006–07 NBA season"), the lighting inside the arena was modified for Lakers games. The lights were focused only on the court itself (hence the promotional _Lights Out_ campaign), reminiscent of the Lakers' early years at [The Forum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Forum_(Inglewood) "The Forum (Inglewood)"). The initial fan reaction was positive and has been a fixture on home games since.[\[55\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-55) The [Daktronics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daktronics "Daktronics") see-through shot clock was first installed prior to the [2008–09 NBA season](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2008%E2%80%9309_NBA_season "2008–09 NBA season").\[ _[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\] The Clippers adopted the new see-through shot clock prior to the [2010–11 NBA season](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2010%E2%80%9311_NBA_season "2010–11 NBA season").\[ _[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\] For Sparks games, the court used is named after Sparks player [Lisa Leslie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lisa_Leslie "Lisa Leslie"), and was officially named prior to the 2009 home opener against the [Shock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Detroit_Shock "Detroit Shock") on June 23, 2006.[\[56\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-56)

The Los Angeles Kings hosted the [2010 NHL entry draft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2010_NHL_entry_draft "2010 NHL entry draft") at the arena in June 2010. In 2012, the Clippers, Kings, and Lakers all advanced to their leagues' respective playoffs,[\[57\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-OCRegister-57) with the Kings ultimately playing their first [Stanley Cup Final](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2012_Stanley_Cup_Final "2012 Stanley Cup Final") at the arena; on June 11, the Kings defeated the [New Jersey Devils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2011%E2%80%9312_New_Jersey_Devils_season "2011–12 New Jersey Devils season") in game six to win their first Stanley Cup in franchise history.[\[58\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-58)

The Lakers unveiled a new hardwood court before their preseason game on October 13, 2012. Taking a cue from [soccer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Association_football "Association football") clubs, the primary center court logo was adorned with 16 stars, representing the first 16 championships the Lakers franchise had won.[\[59\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-59) A 17th star was added to the court and unveiled before their regular season opener on December 22, 2020, to represent the franchise winning its 17th championship in the [2020 NBA Finals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2020_NBA_Finals "2020 NBA Finals").[\[60\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-60)

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Crypto.com_Arena_interior_2024.jpg/330px-Crypto.com_Arena_interior_2024.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Crypto.com_Arena_interior_2024.jpg) During a Lakers game against the [Oklahoma City Thunder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oklahoma_City_Thunder "Oklahoma City Thunder") in November 2024

Crypto.com Arena has hosted the following championship events:

- [NBA Finals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NBA_Finals "NBA Finals"):

  - **[2000](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2000_NBA_Finals "2000 NBA Finals")**: On June 19, 2000, the Lakers defeated the [Indiana Pacers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indiana_Pacers "Indiana Pacers") 116–111 in game 6, which took place at home, to win their twelfth championship title. This was also notable for being their first championship since [1988](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1988_NBA_Finals "1988 NBA Finals").
  - **[2001](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2001_NBA_Finals "2001 NBA Finals")**: The Lakers hosted games 1 and 2 versus the [Philadelphia 76ers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philadelphia_76ers "Philadelphia 76ers"). They eventually won the series in five games.
  - **[2002](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2002_NBA_Finals "2002 NBA Finals")**: The Lakers hosted games 1 and 2 versus the [New Jersey Nets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brooklyn_Nets "Brooklyn Nets"). They eventually swept the series in four games.
  - **[2004](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2004_NBA_Finals "2004 NBA Finals")**: The Lakers hosted games 1 and 2 versus the [Detroit Pistons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Detroit_Pistons "Detroit Pistons"). They eventually lost the series in five games.
  - **[2008](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2008_NBA_Finals "2008 NBA Finals")**: The Lakers hosted games 3, 4 and 5 versus the [Boston Celtics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boston_Celtics "Boston Celtics"). They eventually lost the series in six games.
  - **[2009](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2009_NBA_Finals "2009 NBA Finals")**: The Lakers hosted games 1 and 2 versus the [Orlando Magic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orlando_Magic "Orlando Magic"). They eventually won the series in five games.
  - **[2010](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2010_NBA_Finals "2010 NBA Finals")**: On June 17, 2010, the Lakers defeated the [Boston Celtics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boston_Celtics "Boston Celtics") 83–79 in game 7, which took place at home, to win their sixteenth championship title.
- [Stanley Cup Final](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stanley_Cup_Final "Stanley Cup Final"):

  - **[2012](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2012_Stanley_Cup_Final "2012 Stanley Cup Final")**: On June 11, 2012, the Kings captured their first [Stanley Cup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stanley_Cup "Stanley Cup") in franchise history after defeating the [New Jersey Devils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Jersey_Devils "New Jersey Devils") 6–1 in game 6.[\[61\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-2012StanleyCup-61)
  - **[2014](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2014_Stanley_Cup_Final "2014 Stanley Cup Final")**: On June 13, 2014, the Kings captured their second Stanley Cup in franchise history after defeating the [New York Rangers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_Rangers "New York Rangers") 3–2 in double overtime of game 5.
- [WNBA Finals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WNBA_Finals "WNBA Finals"):

  - **[2001](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2001_WNBA_Championship "2001 WNBA Championship")**: The Sparks hosted game 2 versus the [Charlotte Sting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlotte_Sting "Charlotte Sting") where they captured their first WNBA championship.
  - **[2002](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2002_WNBA_Finals "2002 WNBA Finals")**: The Sparks hosted game 2 versus the [New York Liberty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_Liberty "New York Liberty") where they captured their second WNBA championship.

They eventually won the series in five games to cpature their third WNBA championship.

#### 2028 Summer Olympics and Paralympics

\[ [edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Crypto.com_Arena&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: 2028 Summer Olympics and Paralympics")\]

Crypto.com Arena will host [artistic gymnastics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gymnastics_at_the_Summer_Olympics "Gymnastics at the Summer Olympics"), trampoline and boxing finals during the [2028 Summer Olympics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2028_Summer_Olympics "2028 Summer Olympics").[\[64\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-64) Per [IOC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Olympic_Committee "International Olympic Committee") rules, the venue must be referred to under a generic name for the duration of the Games.[\[65\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-65)

The wheelchair basketball competition will be held at the arena during the [2028 Summer Paralympics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2028_Summer_Paralympics "2028 Summer Paralympics").[\[66\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-66)

### Professional wrestling

\[ [edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Crypto.com_Arena&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Professional wrestling")\]

Along with hosting many episodes of _[Raw](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WWE_Raw "WWE Raw")_ and _[SmackDown](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WWE_SmackDown "WWE SmackDown")_, such as the latter's [20th anniversary season premiere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WWE_SmackDown%27s_20th_Anniversary "WWE SmackDown's 20th Anniversary") on October 4, 2019, Crypto.com Arena has also hosted the following WWE pay-per-views:

- [Judgment Day 2004](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WWE_Judgment_Day_(2004) "WWE Judgment Day (2004)")
- [WrestleMania 21](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WrestleMania_21 "WrestleMania 21")

- [SummerSlam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SummerSlam "SummerSlam") ( [2009](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SummerSlam_(2009) "SummerSlam (2009)"), [2010](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SummerSlam_(2010) "SummerSlam (2010)"), [2011](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SummerSlam_(2011) "SummerSlam (2011)"), [2012](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SummerSlam_(2012) "SummerSlam (2012)"), [2013](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SummerSlam_(2013) "SummerSlam (2013)"), [2014](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SummerSlam_(2014) "SummerSlam (2014)"))

- [No Mercy 2017](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/No_Mercy_(2017) "No Mercy (2017)")

- [NXT Stand & Deliver 2023](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NXT_Stand_%26_Deliver_(2023) "NXT Stand & Deliver (2023)")

A broadcast of _[WCW Monday Nitro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WCW_Monday_Nitro "WCW Monday Nitro")_ was held at the arena on January 24, 2000.

The arena hosted _SmackDown_ and the 2023 [WWE Hall of Fame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WWE_Hall_of_Fame "WWE Hall of Fame") induction ceremony on March 31 as well as [NXT Stand & Deliver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NXT_Stand_%26_Deliver_(2023) "NXT Stand & Deliver (2023)") on April 1 and _Raw_ on April 3, as part of [WrestleMania 39](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WrestleMania_39 "WrestleMania 39") weekend.[\[68\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-68)

### Celebrity memorials

\[ [edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Crypto.com_Arena&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Celebrity memorials")\]

The arena has hosted three public memorials for celebrities.

First, it was for the [memorial of musician Michael Jackson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Jackson_memorial_service "Michael Jackson memorial service") following his [death](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Death_of_Michael_Jackson "Death of Michael Jackson") on June 25, 2009. The memorial took place on July 7, 2009. It included eulogies or performances from [Smokey Robinson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smokey_Robinson "Smokey Robinson"), [Mariah Carey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mariah_Carey "Mariah Carey"), [Macaulay Culkin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macaulay_Culkin "Macaulay Culkin"), [Trey Lorenz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trey_Lorenz "Trey Lorenz"), [Queen Latifah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Queen_Latifah "Queen Latifah"), [Lionel Richie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lionel_Richie "Lionel Richie"), [John Mayer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Mayer "John Mayer"), [Stevie Wonder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stevie_Wonder "Stevie Wonder"), [Kobe Bryant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kobe_Bryant "Kobe Bryant"), [Magic Johnson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magic_Johnson "Magic Johnson"), [Jennifer Hudson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jennifer_Hudson "Jennifer Hudson"), [Berry Gordy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berry_Gordy "Berry Gordy"), [Rev. Jesse Jackson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rev._Jesse_Jackson "Rev. Jesse Jackson"), [Rev. Al Sharpton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rev._Al_Sharpton "Rev. Al Sharpton"), [Brooke Shields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brooke_Shields "Brooke Shields"), [Martin Luther King III](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_Luther_King_III "Martin Luther King III"), [Bernice King](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernice_King "Bernice King"), [Sheila Jackson-Lee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheila_Jackson-Lee "Sheila Jackson-Lee"), [Usher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Usher_(entertainer) "Usher (entertainer)"), [Shaheen Jafargholi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shaheen_Jafargholi "Shaheen Jafargholi"), [Kenny Ortega](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kenny_Ortega "Kenny Ortega"), [Judith Hill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judith_Hill "Judith Hill"), [Orianthi Panagaris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orianthi_Panagaris "Orianthi Panagaris"), his siblings [Marlon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marlon_Jackson "Marlon Jackson"), [Jermaine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jermaine_Jackson "Jermaine Jackson"), and [Janet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Janet_Jackson "Janet Jackson"), and his daughter [Paris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paris_Jackson "Paris Jackson").[\[71\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-71)

In 2019, a memorial took place at the arena for rapper [Nipsey Hussle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nipsey_Hussle "Nipsey Hussle") following his death on March 31, 2019. The memorial service took place on April 11, 2019. Attendees and performers included rapper [YG](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/YG_(rapper) "YG (rapper)"), [Stevie Wonder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stevie_Wonder "Stevie Wonder") who sang "Tears in Heaven", his wife [Lauren London](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lauren_London "Lauren London"), [Jhené Aiko](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jhen%C3%A9_Aiko "Jhené Aiko"), [Anthony Anderson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthony_Anderson "Anthony Anderson"), [Marsha Ambrosius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marsha_Ambrosius "Marsha Ambrosius") and [Snoop Dogg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snoop_Dogg "Snoop Dogg") who gave a eulogy. A hurst procession after the service followed through various [South Los Angeles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Los_Angeles "South Los Angeles") communities.[\[72\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-72)[\[73\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-73)

In 2020, it served as the location for the public memorial of basketball player [Kobe Bryant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kobe_Bryant "Kobe Bryant") following the death of him and his daughter Gianna, among others, in the [2020 Calabasas helicopter crash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2020_Calabasas_helicopter_crash "2020 Calabasas helicopter crash"). It took place on February 24 (2/24, a reference to Gianna's and Kobe's basketball numbers) 2020. It was hosted by [Jimmy Kimmel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jimmy_Kimmel "Jimmy Kimmel") and included eulogies from his wife Vanessa, [Shaquille O'Neal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shaquille_O%27Neal "Shaquille O'Neal"), [Michael Jordan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Jordan "Michael Jordan"), [Rob Pelinka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rob_Pelinka "Rob Pelinka"), [Diana Taurasi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diana_Taurasi "Diana Taurasi"), [Geno Auriemma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geno_Auriemma "Geno Auriemma"), and [Sabrina Ionescu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sabrina_Ionescu "Sabrina Ionescu").[\[74\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crypto.com_Arena#cite_note-74)
